Hatsune Miku The First Sound of the Future

By Maria Viloria | May 2023
Hatsune Miku, as she appears in her latest release, Hatsune Miku NT iXima

If you reside on the anime side of the Internet, you might've occassionally seen a character with long blue pigtails making the rounds on social media. Maybe you've even wondered what anime she's from. And perhaps your curiousity once led you to look her up only to find results for not an anime but a...Vocaloid?

Or maybe you're like most of the rest of the English-speaking world and have no idea what I'm talking about.

Either way this begs the question:

Who is Hatsune Miku?

Well, it turns out there are actually two parts to this question.

Let's talk about Hatsune Miku the software vs Hatsune Miku the character.

Miku, The Software

Hatsune Miku's Vocaloid 2 Box Art KEI

Hatsune Miku is a voicebank for Vocaloid, a voice synthesizer software made by Yamaha. However, it is unlike other voice synthesizers you may be familiar with like text to speech programs, such as Siri on iOS, in that it is made for music production.

In short, Hatsune Miku is a virtual instrument--a virtual singer.

Hatsune Miku was developed by Crypton Future Media and released on Yamaha's Vocaloidd 2 on August 30, 2007. Fujita Saki, a Japanese voice actress, provided her voice, and KEI, a Japanese manga artist, provided her design. She was the first of Crypton's "Character Voice" series and is the most popular Vocaloid to date. It wouldn't be a stretch to say she is the face of Vocaloid itself.

Miku originally only had a Japanese voicebank, but she received an English voicebank on August 31, 2013 and a Mandarin Chinese voicebank in September 2017. Over the years she's additionally received updates to her functionality including different additional voice libraries to expand her emotional expression. The most recent release of Miku on the Vocaloid engine is her 2016 v4x release on Vocaloid 4.

Her most recent release, however, was not on Vocaloid but a November 2020 release on a new voice synthesizer program developed by Crypton called Piapro Studio NT. This marks her first departure from Vocaloid since her release in 2007. Makes you wonder what kind of brand confusion will ensue with her strong association to Vocaloid, but I digress.

Miku, The Character

One might wonder what sort of character Miku must be in order to have so many invested in a piece of software. There's apparently enough to her to write a whole tribute page. That must mean something right?

To start this section off, let's look at Hatsune Miku's official character profile on Crypton's website.

Hatsune Miku's profile on the official Crypton site. Yes this is the whole thing.

It turns out that's all to her entire (official) character! ...Except sometimes people don't follow what's written in the profile either. Maybe you see where this is going.

Hatsune Miku is a sandbox in character form. As an instrument with nothing but a face and a voice, people can do whatever they want with her character. The only limit is really their imagination.

Sure there are a few pieces of official media that depict Miku as a traditional character, like the 2020 mobile rhythm game Hatsune Miku: Colorful Stage developed by Sega. But even in that game, Miku is actually depicted as 5 completely separate entities each with a distinct personality. The form she takes depends on the people interacting with her, and this is true in real life as well.

The Mikus of Hatsune Miku: Colorful Stage. Each one has her own design and personality.

Hatsune Miku, the character, is whoever the fans want her to be. Really, she is what each individual user decides she is.

Biography

In talking about Miku's history as a character, there's nowhere to start but with the beginnings of the Vocaloid community itself. After all, Miku is an instrument devoid of a personality of her own. She needs someone to breathe life into her--to give her something to sing about.

And when discussing the beginnings of Hatsune Miku and the Vocaloid community, you have to talk about Nico Nico Douga, the Japanese video site that was instrumental in propelling Hatsune Miku to stardom. I likely wouldn't be here talking about her over 15 years after her release if not for the community that manifested there.

Nico Nico Douga and the Vocaloid boom

Immediately after her release, people were uploading videos of Miku singing covers of various songs. In fact, on September 3, 2007, just 4 days after her release, Miku had her first viral video, a cover of Ievan Polkka posted by user Otomania. It was a Miku version of the Leekspin meme complete with its own art and video by another user, Tamago.

It became big enough that later on, the version of Miku that appears in this video (dubbed Hachune Miku by fans) was officially recognized by Crypton themselves. This video would additionally cause Miku to become associated with green onions.

But it wouldn't take long for users to start uploading their own completely original works. The earliest original Hatsune Miku song on the site

*warning Contains unsettling eye imagery
was uploaded by user MedamaP on September 8, 2007, about a week after her release.

And 11 days after her first original song, on September 19, 2007, Miku had her first huge hit: Miku Miku ni Shite Ageru (lit. I'll Miku Miku You) by user ika. Remarkably, there were actually relatively popular original songs even before it was uploaded, but this one really took the site by storm.

The original Miku Miku ni Shite Ageru video ika

Something I haven't mentioned yet is the fact that Nico Nico Douga has a ranking of the most popular videos in each category per week. You might think of it as the Vocaloid equivalent of the Billboard Top 100.

About the Vocaloid Rankings

Nowadays there's a separate Vocaloid category, but it didn't exist back in 2007. Even if it did, Nico Nico Douga itself doesn't actually store ranking data from that long ago.

But it turns out the user sippotan has been uploading videos of the weekly Vocaloid ranking since October 2007, using the views, comments, and mylists (functionally equivalent to favorites) of each video to calculate the standings. So when I talk about the Vocaloid rankings during this section, I'm actually referring to this person's videos.

About the Ranking Calculation

The original formula for ranking calculations was:

(views) + (comments) + (mylists * 20)

On week #14 it changed to:

(views) + (comments * weighting) + (mylist * 20), where the weighting was the ratio of the sum of just the views and mylists to the sum of all the stats

This was due to users spamming comments in order to manipulate the ranking.

To this day, they still upload Vocaloid ranking videos despite the existence of the built-in rankings for the Vocaloid category. We wouldn't have such detailed historical data on the state of the fledgling Vocaloid community without them as they list the number of views, comments, and mylists gained over the week for each song within their videos.

Miku Miku ni Shite Ageru was in the top 5 for 16 weeks weeks straight, and stayed in the top 30 for months. It's just a silly, nonsensical electro pop song, but it became a viral meme that spread like wildfire. And like any meme, users created variation upon variation of it. Today it is the most viewed Vocaloid video on the site of all time.

To get a sense for how rapidly the landscape was changing at this time, consider that for the first 3 or 4 weeks, the Vocaloid ranking was mostly covers, but by week 6, there were more original songs than covers, which were becoming increasingly rare to see in the top 30.

The scene during this time was definitely bustling, but it was still very very niche. Most people couldn't get past the robotic sound of Miku's voice enough to enjoy the music.

This would change in December, when user ryo uploaded the song Melt.

The Melt Shock

Melt, the song that many say changed the Vocaloid community ryo

On its initial release, it was popular but not groundbreaking. However, a week after its release user halyosy uploaded his own cover of Melt. This cover became popular, and led to several others posting covers of their own. The Nico Nico Douga rankings (not the Vocaloid ranking but site wide ranking) this week were full of Melt, surprising users outside the community who'd never even heard of the song before. This event became known as the Melt Shock.

The Melt Shock drew in a lot of new fans. In addition to covers, people were making their own music videos for the song, uploading themselves dancing to it, and doing their own arrangements. These videos garnered a lot of attention and led to Melt becoming even more popular.

Music-wise, Melt actually changed the perception of what a Vocaloid song could be. Prior to its upload, most popular Hatsune Miku songs were written to her, about her, or as her speaking to the audience. They were songs about the character, Miku. Melt, however, was not about Miku in particular. It depicts a simple story about someone finally getting to go out with the person they have a huge crush on only for it to start raining in the middle of the date. The story ends happily, with the date going well regardless.

This started a trend toward writing songs using Vocaloids to tell a story rather than writing songs for the Vocaloids themselves. This actually led to an expansion on the range of emotions and types of roles Vocaloids would play in songs.

With this, Miku truly became a character you could do anything with. She could be the forbidden lover of an angel, a robot trying to sprout the last seed that remains in the apocalypse, or Alice in a musical version of Alice in Wonderland. She could sing about feelings of severe depression, about being yourself, about saying goodbye.

Beyond Music

I've mostly talked about the music side of things so far, but perhaps more impressive was really the flood of derivative works based on these songs. Fans of these videos, complete strangers to the original artist, would make things like music videos using their own art or compilations of fanart, human covers of the songs, and even parodies featuring other popular characters of the time including other Vocaloids.

Incredibly, people even began making software related to Vocaloid. There were two notable ones that I'll highlight: MikuMikuDance (MMD), a program for making 3D dance videos using both built in models and models the user could import themselves, and UTAU, essentially a freeware version of Vocaloid which allowed users to create a Vocaloid-like voice synthesizer using their own voice. That is, they could make UTAU, which were voicebanks for a voice synthesizer similar to Vocaloid. Ai Dee, which is the song I linked above about being yourself, has a music video that was made in MMD. As for UTAU, you've probably heard a song using it without realizing as Nyan Cat (yes, the poptart cat meme) is actually an UTAU cover of a Hatsune Miku song called nyanyanyanyanya by daniwellP.

The original Nyan Cat video daniwellP

An important point of all this is these productions were entirely fanmade. No one on the site during this time was commissioned to compose songs or create videos or write software for Miku. Everything was done by just some people on the Internet. And this is what really lies at the heart of the early Vocaloid community--a group of amateur hobbyists creating incredible works for the fun of it.

I could go on and on detailing the evolution of the Vocaloid community on Nico Nico Douga, but then this would become a tribute to the Vocaloid community on Nico Nico Douga, so I'll leave it here. The community would see an influx of talented producers, singers, dancers, illustrators, and animators over the next few years. And all the while, Miku's popularity continued to grow.

Nico Nico Douga would continue to be the central hub for Hatsune Miku and other Vocaloid works until the scene began shifting over to YouTube around 2015. Vocaloid works are still popular on Nico Nico Douga, but it is no longer the only place people are posting their work.

Significant Milestones

In response to Miku's explosive popularity on the Internet, Crypton began heavily marketting Hatsune Miku in 2008, where her original promotion prior to release was limited to information from Crypton themselves and a promotion in Desktop Musician MAGAZINE's November 2007 issue. Thus, she began to appear in many places. Here is a list of some notable milestones for her.

  • 2008-2010 Sponsored by Good Smile Company, who licensed Hatsune Miku, the Studie GLAD Racing team compete in a car featuring Hatsune Miku illustrations all over it.
    The 2008 Miku car
  • 2008 Appeared in the anime Lucky Star. Marks her first appearance in an anime. (This also happens to have been my first exposure to Miku)
  • July 2, 2009 A Vocaloid-focused rhythm game called Project Diva is released. It featured popular Vocaloid songs from the community. This would be the first in a long running series of Project Diva games.
  • August 22, 2009 Performed live (as a projected animation) at Japan's Animelo Summer Live. This was her first performance in a live concert.
  • November 21, 2009 First overseas live performance at Anime Festival Asia in Singapore
  • 2010 Derivative designs of Miku begin to be used during winter festivals in Sapporo, Japan. From 2011 onwards, there was a new unique design, called that year's Snow Miku.
  • March 9, 2010 First solo live: Miku no Hi Kanshasai 39's Giving Day.
  • May 2011 Miku appears in US Toyota Corolla ads. This would be her first appearance on American television.
  • July 2, 2011 US concert debut at Anime Expo in Los Angeles.
  • 2014 Miku's first world tour, Miku Expo, held in Indonesia and the US. Miku Expo would go on to be a yearly tour.
  • March 12, 2014 Collaboration with Japanese band BUMP OF CHICKEN singing 「ray」.
  • May 6-June 3, 2014 Opening act for Lady Gaga's ArtRave world tour
    *warning Contains flashing lights
  • October 8, 2014 Performed on The Late Show with David Letterman, marking her first appearance on US network television.
  • January 2020 Announced as a performer for Coachella 2020. (canceled due to COVID-19
  • September 30, 2020 Hatsune Miku enters the mobile game market with Hatsune Miku: Colorful Stage
  • February 2021 Crypton announces the development of an original animated series related to Hatsune Miku
  • June 6, 2021 Miku Expo 2021 is held online for free, funded by a kickstarter from 2020 in response to cancelation of the in person concert due to COVID-19

Contributions to Society

Miku performing at Miku Expo 2018

Much of Miku's contribution to society lies within the very community that essentially made her what she is today. Hatsune Miku was the starting point for many a creative's journey.

Miku and the Vocaloid Community

It's difficult to put into words the phenomenon that is Hatsune Miku and what a profound impact she's had on people. The closest I've seen to really conveying it is this Google Chrome commercial. I really recommend watching it. I could gush forever about Hatsune Miku and the Vocaloid community and it wouldn't convey half as much as this video does.

A Google Chrome commecial featuring Miku singing Tell Your World kz. (livetune)

Hatsune Miku truly brings people together from all over the world and inspires them to create art. I can say that's 100% true because I've experienced it myself. I've sung together and worked with people literally all over the world to make derivative videos based on Vocaloid songs. I've made friends from Germany, Malaysia, Australia, Peru. All because we enjoy Vocaloid songs. These are connections that were possible only because of Hatsune Miku. And of course, I'm not unique in this.

From Amateurs to Pros

Many who started out posting songs using Hatsune Miku or singing covers of those songs on Nico Nico Douga have gone on to work professionally in the music industry. There are far too many to list, in fact, but here are some notable ones:

  • ryo, who wrote Melt as well as other Vocaloid hits like World is Mine, would quickly break into the professional scene. In 2009, his band, Supercell, debuted with Kimi no Shiranai Monogatari, which was used as the ending song of the anime Bakemonogatari.

  • Gazelle, who gained significant popularity on Nico Nico Douga with her cover of Melt, would later debut as Yanagi Nagi, a singer famous in the anime scene. She provided the vocals to Supercell's Kimi no Shiranai Monogatari and went on to debut as a soloist in 2012.

  • Hachi, writer of Vocaloid hits Matryoshka and DUNE, debuted under his real name Kenshi Yonezu in 2012. He is now one of the biggest pop stars in Japan with several chart-topping hits such as Lemon, Peace Sign (the first opening theme of the anime My Hero Academia), and Kick Back (the opening theme of the anime Chainsaw Man). Many on this list are working in the anime scene, but he's additionally popular even in the mainstream music scene.

  • GARNiDELiA, a duo consisting of Vocaloid producer TokuP and Nico Nico Douga cover artist MARiA, have several hits under their belt including ambiguous, the opening theme of the 2014 anime Kill La Kill.
